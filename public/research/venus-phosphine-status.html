Venus phosphine: detection claim, follow-up constraints, and present status

Review of the 2020 phosphine-detection report at Venus, the published follow-up observations and reanalyses, and the current status of the claim, with focus on the JCMT, ALMA, SOFIA, and recent JWST observations.
2026-04-25 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

The 2020 announcement of a putative phosphine (PH3) detection in the Venus atmosphere triggered intensive observational and theoretical follow-up. Greaves et al. reported a 1.123 mm absorption feature attributed to PH3 in JCMT data and confirmed in ALMA imagery, at mole-fraction abundance approximately 20 ppb in the Venus cloud deck. The interpretation was tentative biotic, on the grounds that no abiotic mechanism in the Venus chemical environment could plausibly produce phosphine at the reported abundance. This report reviews the data record and the present status.

Original detection

The Greaves et al. (2020) detection used the James Clerk Maxwell Telescope (JCMT) on Mauna Kea in 2017, identifying a 4.4-sigma absorption feature at 266.94 GHz attributed to the J=1-0 PH3 rotational transition. The team obtained follow-up observations using the Atacama Large Millimeter Array (ALMA) in 2019, which appeared to confirm the detection at lower significance after extensive data reduction. The paper was published in Nature Astronomy in September 2020 with PH3 mole fraction approximately 20 ppb in the Venus cloud-deck altitude range.

Follow-up reanalyses and reductions

Several teams reanalysed the original data with different reduction pipelines. The most consequential reanalysis was led by Snellen et al. (2020) and Villanueva et al. (2021), who showed that the apparent absorption feature was substantially weaker or absent under standard millimetre-wave reduction practices. Specifically, the bandpass-calibration step used by the original team, designed to remove broad spectral baselines, was found to over-subtract baseline structure in a way that produced apparent absorption features at signal-to-noise levels close to the reported detection.

The Greaves team responded with a re-reduction (Greaves et al. 2021) that retained a marginal signal at lower abundance (approximately 1-7 ppb). The follow-up debate remains active in the published literature.

Independent observation campaigns

Several telescopes have searched for PH3 in independent data sets:

SOFIA. The Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy carried out 2021 observations using its EXES instrument at infrared wavelengths complementary to the original sub-mm bands. The published SOFIA results (Cordiner et al. 2022) report no detection at sensitivity 0.8 ppb at the cloud-deck altitudes consistent with the lower bound of the Greaves re-reduction.

JWST. The James Webb Space Telescope observed Venus in 2024 using the NIRSPEC instrument. Published preliminary analyses do not show a clean detection of PH3 at the cloud-deck altitudes, with upper limits competitive with SOFIA.

Earth-based and balloon instruments. Subsequent campaigns from the IRAM 30-m and other facilities have not produced confirmatory detections at the original abundance.

Theoretical context

The interpretation of any phosphine detection at Venus rests on the absence of plausible abiotic production. Bains et al. (2021) catalogued chemical pathways in the Venus environment and concluded that no chemical pathway involving lightning, volcanic outgassing, photolysis, or surface reactions can produce PH3 above approximately 0.001 ppb. If the abundance is greater than this threshold, the interpretation requires either a previously unrecognised abiotic source or a biotic source. The threshold is well below current observational upper limits, leaving the question of which interpretation applies undecided in principle.

Present status

The phosphine-at-Venus question is currently characterised in the published literature as unresolved. The original detection has not been confirmed by independent observations at the originally reported abundance. The lower-abundance re-reduction by Greaves et al. (2021) is consistent with current upper limits but is not statistically distinguishable from non-detection at the precision of present sub-mm Venus data. The question is one of the leading observational targets for the upcoming DAVINCI mission, which will provide direct in-situ atmospheric chemistry measurements during descent.

Outlook

NASA's DAVINCI descent probe (planned launch 2029, descent 2030) carries the Venus Mass Spectrometer designed to measure atmospheric chemistry including phosphorus species at parts-per-billion sensitivity during the cloud-layer transit. ESA's EnVision orbiter (planned launch 2031) will provide complementary remote-sensing infrared spectroscopy at higher altitudes. The combination will resolve the phosphine question definitively at well-below-1-ppb sensitivity.
