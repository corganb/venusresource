Surface weathering and recent volcanic activity at Maat Mons

Review of the Magellan SAR re-analysis evidence for recent volcanic activity at Maat Mons, the consensus interpretation of vent reshaping between Magellan cycle 1 and cycle 2 imagery, and the implications for current Venus volcanism.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Maat Mons is a 5 km tall shield volcano in the equatorial Atla Regio of Venus. The volcano is the second-tallest mountain on Venus and one of the most prominent volcanic features in the Magellan radar imagery. In 2023, Herrick and Hensley reported the detection of vent-reshaping at Maat Mons between Magellan cycle 1 (1991) and cycle 2 (1992) imagery - the first directly-observed evidence of geologically-recent volcanic activity on Venus. This report reviews the evidence, the interpretive consensus, and the implications for the present-day volcanic state of Venus.

Magellan SAR coverage

The Magellan mission (1989-1994) imaged Venus at 75-150 m resolution using S-band synthetic aperture radar. Three imaging cycles produced overlapping coverage at different solar geometry: cycle 1 (left-look, 1990-1991), cycle 2 (right-look, 1991-1992), and cycle 3 (left-look stereo, 1992-1994). Approximately 25 percent of the surface received cycle 1 and cycle 2 coverage with comparable geometry suitable for change detection. The cycle 3 coverage was geometrically distinct and is used primarily for stereo topography rather than direct change detection.

The Maat Mons vent change

Herrick and Hensley (2023, Science) identified a vent at the summit of Maat Mons that changed shape between cycle 1 and cycle 2 imagery taken approximately 8 months apart. The cycle 1 vent was a roughly circular feature approximately 2.2 sq km in area; the cycle 2 vent had grown to approximately 4 sq km, with a different shape and an associated downslope flow feature. The interpreted change is consistent with a vent enlargement due to a small eruption, with the vent margin reshaped by the eruption and a basaltic flow extending into the surrounding terrain. The interpretation is robust to the modest geometric differences between cycles.

Independent corroboration

Sulburn (2024) and additional follow-up studies have searched the Magellan archive for similar change-detection signatures at other volcanic centres. Two additional candidate change events have been identified at Innini Mons and Sif Mons, with somewhat lower confidence than the Maat Mons detection. The cumulative evidence supports the interpretation that Venus has ongoing low-rate volcanic activity comparable to Earth's continental volcanic provinces, rather than being volcanically dormant. The implied global eruption rate is approximately one substantial event per 1-10 Earth years, depending on the assumed size distribution.

Atmospheric SO2 evidence

Independently of the Magellan change-detection, Venus Express (ESA, 2006-2014) and ground-based observations show variability in atmospheric SO2 abundance over multi-year timescales that has been interpreted as evidence of episodic volcanic outgassing. The SO2 variability does not directly identify specific eruptions but is consistent with the kind of intermittent activity implied by the Maat Mons detection. The Akatsuki mission (JAXA, 2015-) extends the atmospheric SO2 monitoring through 2024.

Surface weathering rates

The Magellan-derived inference of recent volcanic activity is consistent with the comparatively-young surface age of Venus inferred from impact-crater counting. The crater-counting age of approximately 500 million years for the global average surface implies either a recent global resurfacing event or ongoing low-rate volcanic resurfacing - the directly-observed activity at Maat Mons supports the latter. Surface weathering rates inferred from the freshness of volcanic morphology and the absence of older small-crater retention support a present-day surface modification rate consistent with continued volcanism.

Comparison to Earth

Venus has approximately 90 percent of Earth's surface area but produces no ocean ridge volcanism (no plate tectonics) and may produce less subduction-related volcanism (no slab dehydration). The dominant volcanic style is hot-spot and shield-volcano activity reminiscent of Hawaii, with widespread distribution across the planet rather than concentration along plate boundaries. The Maat Mons detection rate, if extrapolated to global volcanic activity, suggests Venus is volcanically less active than Earth but more active than Mars on a per-unit-surface-area basis.

Outlook

Three planned Venus orbital missions will provide direct change-detection of present-day surface activity. NASA's VERITAS mission (currently 2031-launch baseline) carries a high-resolution radar that will repeat the Magellan coverage with order-of-magnitude better resolution and substantially better geometric repeat-pass capability. ESA's EnVision mission (early 2030s launch) carries radar and surface emissivity instrumentation for thermal-anomaly detection. NASA's DAVINCI mission (2031 launch) is a descent probe rather than orbiter but provides complementary atmospheric chemistry context. The combined mission portfolio is expected to confirm or refute the Maat Mons detection and to establish an independent baseline of present-day Venus volcanic activity.
