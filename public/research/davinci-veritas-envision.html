Future Venus mission architecture: DAVINCI, VERITAS, and EnVision

Review of the next-decade Venus mission portfolio - NASA's DAVINCI atmospheric probe and VERITAS radar orbiter, ESA's EnVision orbiter, and the complementary science questions each addresses for surface composition, atmosphere structure, and present-day activity.
2026-04-28 · 6 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

After approximately three decades with no dedicated Venus surface or radar-imaging mission, NASA's 2021 Discovery selection (DAVINCI and VERITAS) and ESA's 2021 Cosmic Vision selection (EnVision) collectively constitute the first major Venus exploration programme since Magellan and Venus Express. The three missions, scheduled for the early 2030s, are designed with deliberate science complementarity. This report reviews each mission's architecture, instrument payload, and science objectives.

DAVINCI

The Deep Atmosphere Venus Investigation of Noble gases, Chemistry, and Imaging mission is a NASA Discovery-class atmospheric descent probe scheduled for 2031 launch. The mission profile includes a flyby phase with multiple flybys past Venus and a descent probe that descends through the atmosphere over approximately one hour. The descent probe carries the Venus Mass Spectrometer (VMS, for noble-gas isotopic composition), the Venus Tunable Laser Spectrometer (VTLS, for trace gases), and the Venus Atmospheric Structure Investigation (VASI, for thermodynamic structure). Imaging instruments include VenDI (visible) for surface imaging during the lower-altitude descent. The probe targets Alpha Regio for surface XRF measurement during the brief sub-cloud-layer-to-surface descent.

Science objectives include the noble-gas inventory (constraining Venus formation and atmospheric escape history), trace-gas vertical structure (constraining atmospheric chemistry), and surface composition at Alpha Regio (constraining the global composition pattern through comparison to the Venera/Vega baseline). The mission also provides the first non-Soviet imagery from below the cloud deck.

VERITAS

The Venus Emissivity, Radio Science, InSAR, Topography, And Spectroscopy mission is a NASA Discovery-class radar orbiter scheduled for 2031 launch. The mission was selected in 2021 alongside DAVINCI but has experienced schedule delays through 2023-2024 NASA cost-and-schedule reviews; the current baseline is 2031 launch. VERITAS carries the VISAR (Venus Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar), which produces 30 m horizontal resolution radar imagery, surface emissivity measurements at six wavelengths, and topographic measurements through interferometry. Repeated coverage of the same surface regions at multiple times provides change-detection sensitivity comparable to that demonstrated for the Magellan archive at Maat Mons.

Science objectives include global topographic mapping at order-of-magnitude better resolution than Magellan, surface composition mapping at lithology scale through the emissivity measurements, and direct change detection of any present-day volcanic or tectonic activity. The mission is the principal data source for the global update to Magellan-era Venus surface knowledge.

EnVision

The EnVision mission is an ESA M5-class Venus orbiter scheduled for 2031 launch as a NASA-ESA partnership. The mission carries a complementary instrument suite: the VenSAR radar (provided by NASA, similar to VERITAS but optimised for cm-class change detection rather than wide-area mapping), the EnVision Subsurface Radar Sounder (SRS, providing first-ever subsurface radar at Venus), the EnVision Spectrometer Suite (VenSpec) for surface and atmospheric composition mapping, and a Radio Science Experiment for gravity-field and atmospheric refractivity measurements.

Science objectives include subsurface structure investigation through the SRS (penetrating 100 m to 1 km depending on terrain), highly-targeted change detection through VenSAR, and atmosphere-surface chemical-coupling investigation through VenSpec. EnVision is the only mission providing subsurface radar sounding at Venus and complements VERITAS through different orbital geometry and instrument balance.

Cross-mission complementarity

The three missions are deliberately complementary in science scope. DAVINCI provides ground-truth surface composition at one site and full atmospheric chemistry. VERITAS provides global topography and surface composition mapping with regional change detection. EnVision provides subsurface structure and targeted change detection. The combined portfolio addresses the principal Venus open questions: the formation history (DAVINCI noble gases), the present surface state (VERITAS topography and composition), the present-day activity (VERITAS and EnVision change detection), and the subsurface structure (EnVision radar sounder).

Scheduling and risk

All three missions have schedule uncertainty. DAVINCI was descoped slightly in the 2024 NASA budget process to maintain the 2031 launch. VERITAS has been re-baselined twice and is at the current 2031 launch baseline. EnVision is on schedule for 2031 launch but has dependencies on the NASA-provided VenSAR. Synchronised arrivals would maximise cross-mission calibration and shared atmospheric-context observation; the current baselines do not strictly require synchronisation but the cross-mission coordination team is working to maintain it.

Outlook

The combined three-mission programme is the largest Venus exploration investment since the Soviet-era Venera programme. The expected science return is comparable in scale to the dramatic improvement in Mars knowledge produced by the post-1996 Mars Exploration Program. Beyond DAVINCI/VERITAS/EnVision, additional Venus missions in concept include the Indian Shukrayaan orbiter (planned 2028) and the Russian Venera-D lander (perpetually in concept-development since the early 2000s). A long-term goal of in-situ surface mobility at Venus remains technically challenging due to the surface temperature; ongoing technology development (high-temperature electronics, balloon platforms) is targeting that capability for the 2040s and beyond.
